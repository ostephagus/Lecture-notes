Suppose we have data $(\vec{X_1}, Y_1), \cdots, (\vec{X_n}, Y_n)$, with $Y_1 \in \R$ and $X_p \in \R^p$.

In a \underline{linear model}, we assume:
\begin{equation*}
    Y_i = \alpha + \beta_1 X_{i,1} + \cdots + \beta_p X_{i,p} + \epsilon_{i}
\end{equation*}
where $\alpha$ is the \underline{intercept}, $(\beta_1, \cdots, \beta_p)$ are the \underline{regression coefficients}, and $\epsilon_1, \cdots, \epsilon_n$ are random noise, with mean $0$ and variance $\sigma^2$.

We want to estimate $\alpha$ and the $\beta_i$. $\vec{X_i}$ are the \underline{predictors} or \underline{explanatory variables}, and $Y_i$ are the \underline{response variables}.
\begin{remarks}
    \item We often eliminate the intercept by defining the first predictor $X_{i1} = 1$ and $\beta_1$ plays the role of $\alpha$.
    \item We can also model non-linear relationships, for example $X_{i1} = z$ and $X_{iz} = z_i^2$, then the linear model is:
        \begin{equation*}
            Y_i = \alpha + \beta_1 z_i + \beta_2 z_i^2 + \epsilon_i
        \end{equation*}
    \item The coefficient $\beta_j$ can be interpreted as the effect on the response of increasing $\vec{X_{ij}}$ by $1$ unit while keeping everything else fixed. In general, however, linear models can only reveal correlations in data rather than causation.
\end{remarks}
We can also consider a matrix formulation. Let $\vec{Y}$ be the vector of all responses, and let $X$ be the matrix with rows $\vec{X_i}$. Then $\vec{\beta}$ is the vector of the $\beta_i$, same with $\vec{\epsilon}$. The linear model is then written as:
\begin{equation*}
    \vec{Y} = X\vec{\beta} + \vec{\epsilon}
\end{equation*}
